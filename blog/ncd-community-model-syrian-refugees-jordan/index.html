A Community-Based Model for Hypertension & Diabetes Among Syrian Refugees in Jordan

An Elrha-funded, IRC-led community health volunteer model for hypertension and diabetes among Syrian refugees in Jordan, supported by a tablet-based CommCare program.

I
International Rescue Committee April 2021 4 min read
Share LinkedIn X

Community health volunteers used a tablet-based CommCare program to manage hypertension and diabetes care in Jordan. Photo: Elrha / T. Fauszt, Mafraq.

Cross-posting this resource — an Elrha-funded research project led by the International Rescue Committee. Read the original project page. The highlights below focus on the role of CommCare.

The International Rescue Committee (IRC), with funding from Elrha, optimized a community health volunteer (CHV) model for non-communicable disease care, specifically hypertension and diabetes, among Syrian refugees in Jordan. A tablet-based CommCare program gave volunteers the tools to identify, monitor, and support patients in the community.

Over a 33-month project (July 2018 to April 2021), CHVs used the CommCare program to monitor medication stockouts, complications, and referral needs, support psychosocial care, and, as the pandemic took hold, screen for suspected COVID-19 cases. The intervention was adapted to remote delivery during COVID-19 disruptions, keeping patients connected to care when in-person visits were limited.

The model achieved strong engagement and clinical outcomes: more than 80% monthly CHV consultation uptake, 87% patient retention in care, and 90% monthly medication adherence, while remaining cost-efficient at roughly INT$218 per patient per year. Disease-control measures held stable through the end of the study.

The project was carried out with research partners including the University of Southern California, the Jordan University of Science and Technology, and Brigham and Women’s Hospital, building evidence for community-based NCD care in humanitarian and refugee settings.
