Improving Polio Vaccine Delivery: How CommCare Powers Campaign Payments

In partnership with WHO and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, CommCare digitized last-mile worker payments for polio and routine immunization campaigns across 16 countries, cutting payment timelines from months to days and reaching some 2 million frontline health workers.

A frontline health worker captures campaign data on CommCare, the first step toward a faster, traceable payment.

In August 2020, global stakeholders gathered to celebrate the near-eradication of wild poliovirus, the result of decades of immunization campaigns. Then COVID-19 hit, redirecting funds and frontline workers away from routine immunization. Within months, years of progress slipped away and polio cases began to rise again. As governments, ministries of health, and funders rallied to re-ignite campaigns, they confronted one of the most stubborn problems in last-mile service delivery: how to pay frontline health workers quickly, fairly, and transparently.

The problem with cash at the last mile

Mobilizing health workers to deliver vaccines at the last mile is pivotal to the success of any mass campaign. Traditionally those workers were paid in cash, a method fraught with complications:

  • Access and security. Many health workers, especially in remote areas, had no bank accounts. Paying them on the spot meant carrying large sums of cash, a serious security risk.
  • Delays in payment. Workers often travelled for hours to collect their pay, and in some cases compensation took a month or more to arrive.
  • Lack of transparency. Cash payments were hard to trace. Workers did not always know how much they were owed, and programs struggled to track what was due versus what was actually paid.

These inefficiencies had a cascading effect. Delayed and incomplete payments demotivated workers and made it harder to recruit them for the next campaign, undermining the timeliness and quality of essential public health services.

A digital solution built on CommCare

Mobile money offered a way out, but deploying it at scale required software to manage the payments. Dimagi worked in collaboration with the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation and the World Health Organization (WHO) to build a tool that streamlines the tracking and management of last-mile payments to campaign workers.

Using CommCare, Dimagi designed a mobile solution that leverages the ubiquity of phones to empower health workers and campaign administrators to deliver vaccinations at the last mile and be paid for it. Before scaling, a pilot tested adoption among 44,815 polio campaign workers, who received payment within eleven days or less, and in some cases the day after a campaign ended. Of 182 workers surveyed, 96 percent said they wanted to be paid via mobile money in future campaigns because the timeliness mattered so much to them.

How the workflow connects

A CommCare app was built for the field campaign managers and administrators on the ground, letting them set up campaigns, enroll workers, verify details, and track attendance across the life of a campaign.

Enrollment and verification

Field managers enroll health workers in the app, capturing demographic information, phone numbers, and the mobile money details needed for payment. Consent is recorded with an electronic signature and stored securely in the CommCare cloud, ready for validation by Mobile Network Operators through the Know Your Customer (KYC) process. The result is a verified database of payment details that lets a program trigger payment the moment a campaign concludes.

Campaign assignment

Ahead of a vaccination event, managers create a campaign defined by its scope, objectives, and duration, then assign each worker a role. The role sets the worker’s responsibilities and the compensation rate they are entitled to.

Attendance tracking

Once a campaign is underway, workers record their attendance in the app as they complete tasks. The data syncs instantly to the CommCare cloud, where administrators review it for accuracy before validated records pass to mobile money administrators, who match each worker against their account and release payment.

The transformative power of mobile money

Mobile money is the connective tissue of the whole approach. Workers without traditional bank accounts can receive compensation directly into their mobile wallets, eliminating long journeys and extended waits to access their earnings. Because Mobile Network Operators validate the payment details, funds reach the intended recipient, and every transaction leaves a clear, traceable trail that reduces the risk of financial mismanagement. Beyond simplifying payday, the model encourages adoption of mobile money and can open doors to greater financial inclusion among underserved populations.

Payment timelines
Months → days

Where compensation once took weeks or months to reach frontline workers, the CommCare solution has cut payment timelines to roughly two weeks, and in some cases a single day, removing the financial strain that once made workers reluctant to sign up again.

Faster pay, stronger campaigns

The impact reaches well beyond individual paydays. Workers who are paid promptly are more likely to take part enthusiastically in future campaigns and to commit to their role in public health. For administrators, centralizing campaign data in CommCare brings real-time insight into the workforce available in a given region, enabling targeted recruitment, more accurate budgeting, and better resource allocation across the herculean logistics of deploying thousands of workers over vast and challenging terrain.

As of February 2025, the program remains active, with results that include:

  • Deployed across 16 countries, including Benin, Burkina Faso, Cameroon, Côte d’Ivoire, Congo, DRC, Gambia, Ghana, Liberia, Nigeria, Sierra Leone, Madagascar, Mozambique, South Sudan, Tanzania, and Togo.
  • 20,000 monthly active users on the CommCare app.
  • Some 2 million health workers enrolled with linked mobile money wallets.
  • Over 2 million paydays facilitated.

A proven, turnkey solution

Dimagi has templatized the solution so it can support campaigns well beyond polio. These templates offer pre-configured features and workflows that teams customize to their own context, drawing on proven approaches built into Dimagi’s deployment methodology. Partners can define a project scope, build a tailored app, deploy it, and train workers in remarkably short timeframes, sometimes as little as two weeks. The same application is already supporting COVID-19 and Yellow Fever campaigns in the DRC, COVID-19 campaigns in Cameroon and South Sudan, and Measles campaigns in Nigeria, with new analytics dashboards, powered by an embedded Apache Superset, and micro-planning tools on the roadmap.
