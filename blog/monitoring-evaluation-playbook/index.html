01, Overview

Goal of This Playbook

Drawing from real-world examples and best practices, this playbook lays out a four-step process for M&E professionals to centralize digital tools within their social impact organizations.

Most social impact organizations recognize the critical need for real-time, actionable data to thrive in today's fast-paced world. Digital tools used for data collection, service delivery, and analysis enable organizations to gather cleaner data more quickly and efficiently, and better drive their Monitoring & Evaluation outcomes.

Organizations commonly grant each project team or country office the autonomy to select the tools best suited to their specific needs, prioritizing four key factors: budget, existing knowledge and skills, donor technical requirements, and project workflow requirements.

While consideration of these factors are crucial, challenges often arise when different projects or country offices procure their own tools based on their own interpretation and priorities around these requirements. A more centralized approach to streamline digital tool selection may align better with your strategic goals, and many social impact organizations have made this transition successfully.

Your organization will be investing in so much more than a technology, they will be investing in a change management process that aims to create a catalytic shift toward accurate and accessible data.

02, Benefits

Why Centralize?

Organizations unlock several key benefits by centralizing their digital tools, while still allowing individual project teams to maintain freedom in customizing applications and surveys.

💲
Reduced Costs

Procuring software project-by-project incurs higher costs than bulk acquisition. Bulk purchasing creates substantial economies of scale, allowing teams to select premium tools at lower cost.

⚡
Greater Efficiencies

Project teams waste time duplicating processes like tool selection and contract negotiations. A central tool enables inter-team learning and better coordinated communities of practice.

📋
Increased Quality

Using different tools across projects makes it impossible to ensure data reliability and cleanliness. A shared tool lets M&E experts better control, coordinate, and manage data across all projects.

🔒
Tighter Security

Ensuring HIPAA, GDPR, and other compliance standards becomes increasingly difficult with multiple tools. A central tool ensures all offices adhere to the highest data protection standards set by HQ.

Step 1 of 4 Build a Task Force

Build a Dedicated Task Force

For the implementation of an organization-wide digital tool, securing buy-in across the entire organization, from country offices to senior leadership, is critical. This is more than a procurement or digital tool change; it is a change management process that needs to be handled carefully.

To ensure success, assemble a multi-disciplinary task force with the expertise and management backing willing to roll up their sleeves and dive into the work. The task force may include members from M&E, Programs, IT, Communications, and ICT4D/digital teams.

The task force's initial steps should include

  • Define clear terms of reference. Clearly articulate the task force's mission, deliverables, and criteria for success, aligned with how results will support the organization and each team.
  • Demonstrate the value proposition. Effectively communicate the "what's in it for me" for each major stakeholder. The selection of this tool should matter to everyone in every department.
  • Build a compelling case for leadership. Convince leadership why they should embrace centralization, addressing the time, effort, and energy the transition will require.
Guidance from real M&E professionals

"Ensure you use the language for each department that is relevant to them when you communicate the tool. For example:

M&E / MEAL May use the tool as a support function for fulfilling donor requirements.
Biz Dev Manage proposal budgets, the source of funding for the tool.
Procurement Can create exceptions that allow integration into long-term agreements and prevent frequent re-tendering.
Sector Leads May want data to understand divisional success and standardized cross-country comparisons.
Comms Need accurate reach data for external communications, good collection tools prevent double-counting."

Step 2 of 4 Needs Assessment

Conduct an In-Depth Needs Assessment

When selecting any software, it's crucial to first understand your users' roles, frustrations, and priorities. Introducing a tool without considering the experiences of the teams that will utilize it is a recipe for failure.

Three simple steps

  1. Dedicate time to gain insights into the challenges teams face and their desired features.
  2. Maintain ongoing communication and involvement with users throughout the process.
  3. Consider the future needs of your organization, not only what would be beneficial today, but what will be necessary in 3, 5, or 10 years.

In addition to this user-centered approach, assess the organization's readiness for a centralized tool. Investigate whether previous attempts have been made and, if so, what led to their failure. Engage with individuals who were involved to understand the reasons, this will help anticipate potential roadblocks.

Decide who to engage

It's critical to understand the needs of staff at every level. Recognize that the requirements of a country director differ from those of a project-level administrator, and these vary substantially from the needs of a field worker. Consider team affiliation (M&E, IT, ICT4D, Program Delivery), functional role, and organizational level (end-user, project, country, regional, or HQ).

Map out what you know, and don't know

The task force should build a comprehensive understanding of the digital tools currently used across the organization. Online surveys are an easy way to collect feedback and gauge satisfaction. This survey should cover the entire data lifecycle:

Data
Collection
Analysis
Visualization
Mapping
Result
Communication

Most task forces also use the feedback to select a diverse group of users from various global offices to form focus groups or conduct interviews, developing deeper insights into their experiences, from highly proficient users to those with limited or unsatisfactory tool experiences.

Key questions your research should answer

  • What tools are program teams already using for data collection, visualization, analysis, and communication?
  • What are the training requirements for programs, M&E, IT, and ICT4D staff?
  • What pain points and issues are associated with current tools?
  • What aspects of the existing system are functioning well and should be retained?
  • What are the fundamental features necessary in a global tool that would support country offices of all sizes?
  • What would you need from a tool in the next 3 years?

Share your final recommendation

With insights from the organization-wide survey and qualitative feedback from focus groups and interviews, create a final report to share with key decision-makers. This report should outline pain points, offer well-considered recommendations, and include a comprehensive list of requirements developed in collaboration with compliance, finance, IT, and procurement teams.

Guidance from real M&E professionals

"Our organization undertook a similar project and estimated a total workload of approximately 200 hours, with responsibilities such as program oversight, meeting coordination, white paper drafting and editing, and extensive discussions and interviews spanning 12 countries.

This sounds like a lot of work, and it is.

But it's essential to invest the time to do this right, because the opportunity cost was much higher."

Step 3 of 4 Select Partner & Tool

Select the Right Partner & Tool

With problems and requirements identified, the next step involves selecting the right technology providers to partner with and establishing a contract that caters to the needs of HQ, country, and project-level users.

Hold discussions with different teams

Collaboration with finance, compliance, programs, business development, and procurement teams is crucial to determining the procurement and billing process of a global tool. Some organizations fund the tool centrally with non-restricted funding; others allocate cost to program or country teams. Aligning the centralization effort with organizational policies ensures smoother operations and integration.

Identify potential technology providers

The vendor should be more than just a technology provider, they should be a true partner, willing to guide the organization throughout the process. Look for a vendor with extensive enterprise experience and a long-term commitment to your organization's institutional needs.

The organization could engage potential vendors in three key ways:

Issue a Tender / RFP The RFP should meticulously outline the organization's requirements and request vendors to address each one comprehensively.
Hire a Consultant This person will lead an evaluation of existing solutions and provide a final recommendation after extensive discussions with vendors.
Hold Interviews / Demos The task force can request demos with vendors they are interested in. This is an opportunity to ask whether their software meets the requirements, including budget, and compare offerings.

Select your tool

Making your final tool selection will feel like a big decision with many inputs to consider. Always keep the bigger picture in mind: what are the main problems you and your team want to solve? Use the four benefits of centralization as your framework:

The tool should be affordable and cost-efficient, particularly.

Understand the costs of both the core digital tool and supplementary expenses (data warehousing, BI tool integration, self-hosting, etc). See Dimagi's Total Cost of Ownership model.

The tool should be publicly accessible and easy to start using.

You're selecting not only the technology but the entire support ecosystem. Review their documentation, is it easy to follow? Submit a support ticket to test their response quality.

The tool should cater to projects of all sizes and enable replicable use cases.

Any tool selected should scale as projects grow and adapt for different use cases. Can an app built for CHWs in Ethiopia be localized for India? Third-party evaluations may help here.

Above all else, the tool must be secure.

Adherence to the most stringent data security and privacy standards is non-negotiable. The tool should be verified by third-party standards including HIPAA, SOC 2, or others.

Guidance from real M&E professionals

"We selected a tool that worked for a global M&E survey, across country offices.

It allowed us to build the surveys ourselves rather than rely on a consultant, which had been significantly limiting.

Now the country offices are rolling out the platform on an as-needed basis, when they have demand, the tool is ready and the learning resources are there for them to build their own apps and surveys."

Step 4 of 4 Roll Out Your Tool

Successfully Roll Out Your Tool

To successfully scale up the chosen tool internally, recognize that this process takes time and sustained effort. Here are the key steps an organization can take to ensure a smooth and effective scale-up.

Rollout considerations
Appoint a Dedicated Owner Designate a responsible individual as the primary contact for the service provider. This owner fields inquiries, consolidates feedback, and relays it to the provider, preventing redundant queries and missed responses. There may also be a dedicated application owner per country office or project.
Establish a Help Desk Create a structured feedback mechanism for users to connect with the application owner. This could be a dedicated Slack or Teams channel, an exclusive email account, or regular check-in calls. The application owner should have KPIs aligned with prompt response times.
Announce the New Tool Ensure the entire organization is informed about the shift from project-based tools to the globally procured platform. Share the advantages and provide a reasonable timeline for projects and country offices to transition.
Designate Country Onboardings Collaborate with country and regional offices to ensure they understand the necessary processes for securing accounts, billing for the tool, and any usage restrictions that may impact budget or usability.
Develop Guidance Materials Written guides, videos, and interactive materials need to be organization-specific, accounting for Standard Operating Procedures and data protection policies. Develop these over time as the organization incorporates more complex features.
Conduct Ongoing Trainings Training sessions hosted by the application owner may include the technology provider. Incorporate multiple mediums, videos, webinars, documents, and in-person sessions, to serve different learning styles. Cascade training through country office product owners.
Guidance from real M&E professionals

"Previously, new programs would approach HQ's M&E team when they wanted to use a centrally procured data collection tool.

The M&E person at HQ would build the app or survey for each project and request, which became unsustainable. There was no capacity building at the country level, and poor data practices were entrenched, excel exports, workarounds, and so on.

We reversed this by hosting weekly (and now monthly) sessions and developed over 50 hours of training on how to use the platform. It took 2.5 years.

It's not just a system, it's a process, that is now saving time and leading to cleaner data."

"
Closing

Final Thoughts

Centralization: Significant Investment, Incredible Returns

There is no question that centralization is a time-intensive effort. It will require up-front investment and time from across your organization to do it successfully. Yet while the initial period may take more time, it can have enormous, positive impacts on an organization, and save significant time down the road.

Although the steps outlined in this playbook may not be universally applicable to every organization, the general guidelines offer a foundational framework for an inclusive and thorough process of procuring a centralized digital tool.

Investing time in careful preparation and collaboration with your team from the outset will yield benefits in terms of efficiency, security, budgeting, and visibility. By involving global teams in the feedback, selection, and onboarding phases, you build their buy-in to the ultimately procured tool, transforming them into enthusiastic champions of its use.
