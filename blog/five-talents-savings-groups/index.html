How Savings Groups Are Shaping Economic and Social Change: A Five Talents Case Study

How 20 years of savings-group data in Burundi, and real-time CommCare data in Kenya, reveal what truly sustainable, community-owned impact looks like.

RL
Rachel Lindley Jul 2025 6 min read
Share LinkedIn X

Image courtesy of Taking Pictures, Changing Lives Foundation. This case study originally appeared in Philanthropy Impact Magazine.

When we seek to create lasting change, it’s the snapshot of a woman in Burundi who learned to read and write, now runs a small business, and sends her daughters to school that grabs attention, far more than any carefully crafted description of what we do.

“Now that I can read, I was elected as a women’s representative in our community. I also took a loan from my Savings Group to buy goats, and I will sell one to pay school fees for my children. No-one can underestimate me now.”

A Five Talents Savings Group member, Burundi

These individual transformations are replicated tens of thousands of times across central and eastern Africa through Five Talents’ programme partnerships. But individual stories aside, broader systemic change matters too. Our donors are keen to understand how those stories lead to deeper, truly sustainable impact: transformation owned by programme members themselves, reaching beyond the individual to their family, community, and future generations.

Two decades of measurable change

In 2022 we commissioned an independent evaluation of our programme in Burundi. With baseline data from when the project started as a Mothers’ Union literacy programme 20 years ago, the report charted cumulative impacts over two decades, across literacy, poverty alleviation, girls’ education, sustainable savings groups, employment, gender equality, and community cohesion.

Learned to read and write since inception
144,000+

78% of them women, accounting for 2.9% of Burundi’s adult literate population. Once literate, participants formed more than 6,200 community-owned savings groups, and over 84% kept operating independently for more than 10 years after the 2-year funding period ended.

That sustainable anchor point means:

  • Almost all members now employ at least one other person; over a third of female and nearly half of male participants have started businesses employing two or more people.
  • More girls are in school: 91% of female and 94% of male participants reported sending children to school in 2022, up from 62% and 52% in 2006.
  • Higher incomes let participants invest in their households, with knock-on benefits for children’s education, housing, and healthcare.

Real-time impact data with CommCare

Alongside the longitudinal evaluation in Burundi, a project with our colleagues in Kenya uses CommCare to document, understand, and deepen impact more quickly. Instead of paper, field staff record members, meetings, and training attendance on a tablet or phone, automatically updating a central dashboard in real time. CommCare works offline, crucial in remote, rural communities, then syncs when a connection is available.

Trainers also use a free-text function to capture qualitative data; the system flags keywords so field staff can spot trends, investigate challenges, and share what’s working, refocusing limited resources where they’ll have the greatest impact.

“From a data quality perspective, CommCare allows us to validate field data. It allows us to set up a system of integrity. From the central office, we can see the penetration of a particular training and identify challenges. When USAID came, they were able to verify the data by ensuring that it matched what was recorded elsewhere.”

Five Talents programme leader, Kenya

Replicating what works

In Tsavo, our partners developed a new training curriculum on environmental conservation. Using CommCare, they now track both the delivery and the impact of each module, assessing effectiveness quickly, making changes as needed, and building evidence to showcase climate-smart training to peers and funders, so others can replicate it faster.

Both the Burundi evaluation and the Kenya data point the way to showcasing truly sustainable impact: where transformation endures, multiplies, and stays in the hands of the communities themselves.
