Malaria Consortium upSCALE: iCCM for Improving Child Health

How Malaria Consortium’s upSCALE program used CommCare for integrated community case management (iCCM), helping community health workers improve child health in Mozambique.

D
Dimagi Sep 2020 3 min read Case Study
Share LinkedIn X

A community health worker uses the upSCALE app to guide care in Mozambique.

Each year, an estimated 5.4 million children under five lose their lives to diseases that are preventable and treatable, including malaria, pneumonia, and diarrhea. In Mozambique, where 48% of the country has no access to healthcare, community health workers known as Agentes Polivalentes Elementares (APEs) are often the only point of contact for families. Malaria Consortium built upSCALE on CommCare to put reliable diagnostic tools in their hands.

Two interlinked problems at the last mile

Community health workers lacked reliable tools to guide them through complex diagnostic protocols, leading to frequent misdiagnosis, incorrect treatment, and unnecessary referrals. Supervisors had no real-time visibility into worker performance or community health status, making it impossible to provide timely support or catch problems early.

Even when workers did collect health data, it had no path into national reporting systems. CommCare was used in the field, while Mozambique’s Ministry of Health relied on DHIS2 for national indicators, with no connection between the two. As a result, community-level data was routinely deprioritized in national planning, leaving policymakers without the granular information needed to direct resources effectively.

A connected system for iCCM

Building on WHO protocols, Malaria Consortium, in partnership with the Ministry of Health and UNICEF, developed upSCALE on CommCare to address both challenges at once. Integrated community case management (iCCM) has demonstrated the potential to reduce childhood mortality by 60%, and upSCALE was built to deliver that while connecting community health data to Mozambique’s national reporting infrastructure.

The community health worker application guides more than 1,200 APEs through the diagnostic process for diarrhea, pneumonia, malaria, and malnutrition using iCCM standard protocols. It is patient-focused rather than disease-focused, promoting continuous care and follow-up on danger signs, risks, and referrals. Its features include:

  • A checklist of danger signs for pregnant women, newborns, and children.
  • A built-in Breath Counter for measuring respiratory rate to diagnose pneumonia.
  • A stock management module to flag low medication supplies.
  • Family planning, antenatal, and postnatal services integrated into the same workflow.
  • SMS notifications connecting workers and supervisors in real time.

A separate supervisory application gives more than 300 facility-level supervisors automated performance data, enabling monthly scoring visits and progress tracking against the national rubric for case management.

Childhood mortality reduction potential
Up to 60%

Integrated community case management has demonstrated the potential to reduce childhood mortality by 60%, the outcome upSCALE was built to deliver across rural Mozambique.

From a worker’s phone to the national dashboard

Dimagi used MOTECH, an open-source data integration platform, to build a seamless two-way connection between CommCare and Mozambique’s DHIS2 national health instance. Data flows automatically from field submissions to national dashboards without manual transfer, so the same information is accessible at every level of the system: workers see a monthly summary of their own activity inside the app, supervisors track performance alongside medication stock, and Ministry M&E teams visualize data at district, provincial, and national level.

DHIS2 is deployed in more than 40 countries. The CommCare integration built for upSCALE creates a replicable model for feeding community health data into national information systems wherever both platforms are used.

Health outcomes at community and national scale

By connecting frontline care to national reporting, upSCALE improved outcomes for the families APEs serve while making community health visible to the people who plan and fund the system. Across the program, the results include:

  • 29,402 children under five receiving health care services.
  • 106,816 community consultations supported directly by the program.
  • 3,110 pregnant women supported through the program.
  • More than 1,500 active workers and supervisors using the platform, with 8,000 workers in the national expansion plan.

With roughly 32% of Mozambique’s population currently served, upSCALE shows how digital iCCM tools can raise the quality of frontline care and bring community data into national decision-making at the same time.
