Terre des hommes: The Integrated eDiagnostic Approach for Child Health

How Terre des hommes deployed an integrated electronic diagnostic approach on CommCare to improve the quality of frontline child healthcare across 1,132 clinics in Burkina Faso.

D
Dimagi Jan 2016 4 min read
Share LinkedIn X

A health worker uses the IeDA tablet application during a child consultation in Burkina Faso.

As part of their Integrated eDiagnostic Approach, Terre des hommes partnered with Dimagi to create a tablet-based application known as the Electronic Register of Consultations (REC). Built on Dimagi’s CommCare platform, the REC increases nurses’ adherence to Integrated Management of Childhood Illnesses (IMCI) protocols and improves their quality of care through enhanced decision support and case management.

The problem: protocols that go unused

According to the World Bank, the child mortality rate in Burkina Faso is more than six times higher than the UN’s target. Most of these deaths are preventable and stem from a lack of access to functioning health services that could have treated common pathologies such as malaria, diarrhea, acute respiratory infections, measles, and acute malnutrition.

The IMCI strategy, a systematic approach to diagnosing child illness in low-resource settings, is used in more than 75 countries to address these issues. Yet fewer than 30% of clinics actually applied it. Nurses in these clinics often face high patient volumes, limited literacy, and little time to complete diagnostic reports, all of which discourage adherence to time-consuming, technical IMCI protocols.

The solution: a tablet app that guides every consultation

The REC mobile health system is built on CommCare and includes a tablet-based mobile application and a web dashboard. The system guides nurses through routine consultations of patients under five years old, using decision support to apply IMCI and reduce diagnostic and treatment errors. Nurses register patients, record vital signs, and enter relevant information in response to the application’s prompts. At each successive consult, the nurse updates the patient’s information and is prompted with the immediate steps to take.

  • Diagnosis according to the IMCI protocol.
  • Automatic posology based on the IMCI and the presentation of symptoms.
  • A simplified user interface with an intuitive workflow and easy learning curve.
  • Data validation mechanisms to ensure data quality.
  • Comparative data visualizations and warning-sign notifications.

The REC also uses the WHO’s Child Growth Standards to determine measures such as weight-for-height, weight-for-age, height-for-age, and mid-upper arm circumference. After a nurse enters a child’s height, weight, temperature, and arm circumference, the app displays a z-score and signals whether the child’s nutritional condition is “good,” “bad,” or in “danger,” helping the nurse quickly identify severe or moderate malnutrition and proceed with appropriate care.

Scaling from a pilot to nationwide deployment

Implementation began in 2014. All system design and field-testing took place in the first year, in preparation for a rollout to 100 primary health clinics reaching 175,000 children. The REC has since been deployed to 1,132 health facilities, where more than 4,800 health workers use it to treat around 200,000 children every month. The program has been so successful that it is being transitioned to Ministry of Health ownership.

Beyond expanding the application’s scope to ante- and post-natal care, Dimagi and Terre des hommes are exploring built-in training and refresher modules. They are also in the early stages of adding AI capabilities to give community health workers real-time recommendations based on their performance history, along with smart dashboards and predictive models for epidemiological surveillance.

The results: better diagnosis, better outcomes

Across facilities using IeDA, 97% of consultations with children under five now use the REC tool, up from a baseline of fewer than 30%. That shift has produced measurable gains in the quality of care:

  • A 50% improvement in adherence to IMCI protocol.
  • Nearly 20% higher accuracy in diagnosing diarrhea, dysentery, and malnutrition.
  • False-positive pneumonia diagnoses down by over 60% and antibiotic over-prescription down 25%.
  • An estimated $32–$66 in cost savings per facility per month through reduced training time, paper consumption, and data submission overhead.

Since launch, IeDA has reached 2.4 million children across more than 5 million consultations, putting decision support directly in the hands of frontline workers and steadily raising the standard of child healthcare nationwide.
