Transforming Community Health: Precision, Prevention, and the Power of Predictive Analytics

How a machine-learning model helps frontline workers in India spot the children at risk of malnutrition before it happens, shifting community health from reaction to prevention.

ND
Nisha Sinha, Dhivya Sivaramakrishnan & Anna Dixon Dec 2025 9 min read AI for Good
Share LinkedIn X

Growth monitoring at a community health centre, where early signals of nutritional risk are easy to miss.

At an Anganwadi Centre in India, a single frontline worker may be responsible for monitoring the growth of hundreds of children at once. The hardest part is not measuring height and weight; it is knowing which child is quietly slipping toward malnutrition before the decline becomes visible. We set out to answer a different question: what if we could anticipate that decline before it happens?

A shift to precision using predictive analytics

Child malnutrition remains one of India’s most stubborn public health challenges. According to NFHS-5 (2019–21), 32.1% of children under five are underweight, 35.5% are stunted, and 19.3% are wasted. Frontline workers carry the weight of responding to these numbers with limited time and resources, and uniform outreach means the children most at risk are not always the ones who get attention first.

“What if we could anticipate nutritional decline before it happens? What if we could help frontline workers focus on children who need attention right now, not weeks later?”

The questions behind the SNEH predictive analytics project

Initially supported by USAID’s Development Innovation Ventures and later completed with funding from Open Road Impact, the project paired Dimagi with the American India Foundation to build a machine-learning model using data from the SNEH program in Madhya Pradesh and Odisha. The goal shifted from identifying children who were already malnourished to predicting the transition from healthy to malnourished, while there was still time to act.

How we built the predictive model

A dataset rooted in real life

The model analyzed data from 22,767 children between 6 and 60 months of age, tracked over 18 months. It drew on four kinds of real, field-collected records:

  • Height and weight measurements.
  • Family demographics and registration.
  • Nutrition supplement records.
  • Home visit observations.

Thinking in time, not just snapshots

Rather than reading a single measurement in isolation, the model looked at each child’s growth trajectory, using 120 days of past data to build its features and looking 60 days into the future to predict a transition. Growth is a story over time, and the trend matters more than any one data point.

Predicting four critical transitions

Among children who were not yet severely malnourished, the model predicted four types of nutritional transition:

  • Stunting transition: normal height-for-age becoming stunted.
  • Wasting transition: normal weight-for-height becoming wasted.
  • Becoming underweight: normal weight-for-age becoming underweight.
  • Underweight worsening: a further decline in weight-for-age.

Rather than maintaining a separate model for each transition, the team built a single unified model, keeping the output simple enough for frontline teams to interpret and trust.

The technology behind the scenes

The team used a Random Forest Classifier in scikit-learn because it is easy to interpret, robust against the noisy data that real field collection produces, and well suited to the non-linear interactions in children’s growth. From an initial set of 54 variables, the final model used 26 carefully selected features.

What the model found

The clearest finding was also the most practical: recent growth patterns were far more predictive than static characteristics. A child’s height-for-age and weight-for-age scores over the previous few months carried the most signal, while factors like parental education, gender, caste, number of siblings, and home visits weighed much less.

“While experience-based judgement is valuable, it does not always capture early and subtle signs of nutritional risk.”

From the SNEH predictive analytics findings

That is exactly where the model adds value. It can detect small shifts in growth, the kind that follow an illness or a stretch of low dietary diversity, before they are obvious to the eye.

How the model performed

Prediction accuracy
3.4× better than chance

On a holdout dataset, the model reached an average precision of 0.53, a 3.4-fold improvement over a random baseline of 0.16. In the field, it flagged at-risk children far more reliably than subjective judgement alone.

No model is perfect, and this one was not either. But even modest gains in precision can translate into thousands of children receiving timely support, which is the difference that matters when resources are limited and time is short.

From predictions to people: what happened in the field

The model surfaced the four highest-risk children at each Anganwadi Centre. Integrated Community Facilitators then focused their efforts on those children, providing:

  • Tailored nutrition counselling.
  • Awareness-raising about the importance of growth monitoring.
  • Behavior-change guidance for caregivers.
  • Continued follow-up and problem-solving support.

This shifted frontline work from uniform outreach to precision counselling, making limited time more meaningful and helping support reach children sooner.

What we learned along the way

1

Collaboration matters more than code

Technical and nutrition experts had to work together throughout. Neither the data scientists nor the program teams could have reached a usable result alone.

2

Accuracy isn’t everything; usability wins

A model is only useful if a frontline worker can act on it. We prioritized simple output, a clear ranking, and actionable insight over chasing maximum theoretical accuracy.

3

Context is everything

A model trained in two states needs a base architecture that can be fine-tuned regionally with local data before it can travel to new geographies.

Implications for public health

Beyond this one program, the work points to a broader shift in how community health can operate:

  • Predictive analytics can support more objective identification of vulnerability than subjective judgement alone.
  • Prevention-first systems are possible when we intervene earlier instead of waiting for nutritional status to deteriorate.
  • Risk scores help frontline workers focus limited time where it is needed most, reducing burden while increasing impact.
  • Identifying absolute risk, not just a relative ranking, helps ensure the most vulnerable children are consistently reached.

Similar approaches could extend to pregnancy-related risks, disease surveillance, and service-delivery monitoring across public health programs.

“This isn’t just about algorithms, it’s about giving every child a fair chance to grow, thrive, and stay healthy. And sometimes, all it takes is noticing the small signals, just a little sooner.”

We see this as a starting point, not a finished product. The work continues: refining the model, improving its generalisability, and exploring how approaches like this can be responsibly integrated into government systems.
