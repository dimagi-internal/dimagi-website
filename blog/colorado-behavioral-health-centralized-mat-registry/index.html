Streamlining Collaborative Care: A Colorado Behavioral Health Case Study

How Colorado’s Behavioral Health Administration partnered with Dimagi to build a centralized CommCare registry for medication assisted therapy, so 97% of client admissions no longer require state intervention.

D
Dimagi May 2023 4 min read
Share LinkedIn X

Colorado’s Behavioral Health Task Force introduces the six pillars of a strong, people-first behavioral health system.

Colorado’s Behavioral Health Administration (BHA) is a cabinet member-led agency within the State’s Department of Human Services, created to drive care coordination across the many state agencies that address behavioral health needs. The BHA became operational on July 1, 2022, with the passage of Colorado House Bill 22-1278, which established its work as a multi-year, phased effort grounded in ongoing partner and community engagement. As part of that mission, the BHA partnered with Dimagi to build a centralized registry of every individual in the state receiving medication assisted therapy (MAT), one that would facilitate care coordination and reporting through a single, unified user experience.

The problem: fragmented, manual intake

Before the BHA formed in 2021, statewide efforts to coordinate MAT care and intake were largely fragmented. Without a central registry accessible to providers, intake was a cumbersome, time-consuming process that required state intervention and hours of stitching together pieces of information from a wide array of sources.

A provider trying to confirm that someone was eligible for intake first had to email a client admission request to state administrators. Those administrators would then query a legacy registry, accessible only to the State, to verify the client was not already in treatment elsewhere. They were responsible for spotting potential admission conflicts and contacting the relevant facilities to confirm their data was current, all while clients in crisis waited. Duplicate enrollments, where a single client received controlled medications from more than one clinic, were common and slow to resolve.

Because data arrived in many formats from many sources, including PDFs, proprietary MAT registries, and Excel spreadsheets, synthesizing it for analysis and decision-making was extremely difficult. That strained already limited resources and left clients waiting longer for the care they needed.

The approach: a centralized registry on CommCare

Colorado partnered with Dimagi in late 2021 with three clear goals:

  • Implement a centralized client registry that delivers an outstanding end-user experience while easing provider reporting burdens and administrative duties.
  • Significantly reduce the time it takes clients to receive the most appropriate care, and in doing so improve behavioral health outcomes.
  • Ease regulatory reporting by automating administrators’ quarterly and annual reports.

Dimagi drew on its extensive experience configuring complex tools on the flexible, modular CommCare platform. Over several months, the application development team worked alongside State subject matter and UX experts to design, build, and deploy a Central Registry with role-based experiences for providers and state users, smart forms that enable targeted conversations and decision support, de-duplication, and other features that support the critical work of MAT client care.

To give users real-time oversight, Dimagi incorporated ancillary tools, including Snowflake and Tableau’s data-visualization solution, within the CommCare framework to create embedded dashboards. Registry users can now monitor metrics such as:

  • Yearly and monthly admission trends: providers and state admins track trends in admissions and discharges over time.
  • Client census: providers see who is admitted and discharged by provider network and OTP clinic, drilling down into the full clinical history of every individual in their care.
  • Average length of stay: providers and state administrators monitor inpatient trends, including clinics with an average length of stay of more than 90 days.
  • Potential duplicates: state administrators are alerted to possible duplicate records so they can clean and reconcile the data.

The BHA Central Registry is also interoperable with other state systems, synthesizing data into an ingestible format across a single dashboard interface.

The results: faster care, less manual work

In the months since the BHA began rolling the registry out to state administrators and providers, feedback has been overwhelmingly positive:

  • CommCare drastically reduced the time it took administrators to query the registry on a provider’s behalf. Because the system is far more usable, state agencies no longer spend hours querying databases.
  • The platform alerts users to potential duplicates, eliminating the need to comb through data by hand.
  • Providers can now query the MAT registry independently, without state intervention, which has sped up the client intake and admission process.
Admissions needing no state involvement
97%

More than 5,700 clients were admitted to MAT programs in the registry’s first 10 months, and 97% of those admissions did not require state team involvement, compared to 0% before the rollout. That is roughly 500 times every month a client was admitted to treatment without waiting on a state team to query on a clinic’s behalf.

Sustainability and what comes next

Dimagi and its partners treat sustainability as a core component of the technology stack, designing tools that future administrators can pick up and run with on their own.

“With everything that Dimagi is building, we’re ensuring that things are as simple as possible and as understandable as possible, so future administrators can use it with ease, even with little to no technology background or training.”

Dimagi

Looking ahead, Dimagi will continue to partner with the BHA to roll out new tools, including a provider directory, a bed capacity tracker, and a referral system. Together these will let providers across the state interface directly with one another, see which services facilities are offering, and make rapid referrals, improving coordination of care and helping the state allocate resources more effectively.
