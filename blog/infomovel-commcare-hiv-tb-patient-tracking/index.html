Infomovel: Tracking HIV and TB Patients Across Mozambique

Infomovel connected more than 1,750 community health workers across seven provinces of Mozambique with CommCare, reducing loss to follow-up and aligning fragmented partners around the UNAIDS 90-90-90 targets for HIV and TB.

D

Share LinkedIn X

A community health worker uses Infomovel on CommCare to track HIV and TB patients in Mozambique.

Sub-Saharan Africa carries the heaviest burden of HIV/AIDS in the world, accounting for more than 70% of global infections. In Mozambique alone, UNAIDS estimated that 2.2 million people were living with HIV in 2018, while roughly 90% of Mozambicans live in an underserved primary health care area. Infomovel, a CommCare-based mobile tool, was built to help frontline workers keep those patients in care.

A crisis paper systems could not keep up with

Tuberculosis deepens the challenge. An estimated 56% of TB patients in Mozambique are co-infected with HIV, and as multi-drug resistant TB rates climbed toward 3–4%, consistent treatment supervision became essential. Yet the country’s TB detection rate barely reached 37%, leaving infections to spread among an overburdened, often undertrained health workforce.

Without digital tracking, patients fell through the cracks. People missed appointments, defaulted on treatment, and continued transmitting disease. Multiple implementing partners worked in different regions with incompatible workflows and siloed data, making it impossible to build a coherent national picture of HIV and TB outcomes.

Designing for the 90-90-90 framework

Led by UNAIDS, the global public health community rallied around a set of targets known as 90-90-90: 90% of people living with HIV should know their status, 90% of those diagnosed should receive sustained antiretroviral therapy, and 90% of those on treatment should achieve viral suppression. In 2016, Mozambique committed to these goals and began treating people with ART immediately after a positive test.

To support community health workers and improve treatment, adherence, and retention, the Ministry of Health, CDC, and USAID engaged Dimagi to design Infomovel, a CommCare-based mobile tool purpose-built to reduce loss to follow-up. Infomovel delivered:

  • Electronic patient registries with GPS integration and unique patient ID features.
  • Longitudinal tracking of people with HIV/AIDS and TB from diagnosis through treatment and end of care.
  • Prioritization tools that surface patients based on adherence, due dates for tests, and missed deadlines for re-testing or appointments.
  • Automatic reminders that trigger follow-up notifications for both patients and providers.

Two applications, three roles, one care system

Infomovel consists of two applications, Health Facility and Community, supporting patient registration, follow-up, defaulter tracking, and household testing. The system links community workers with health facility focal points through case management for HIV testing and counseling, care and treatment, PMTCT, and TB workflows. Three user roles keep that system coordinated:

  • Case Managers connect information between the health facility and community workers, managing HIV/TB service delivery and links to the OpenMRS and ePTS health information databases.
  • Counsellors (Conselheiros) are CHWs who deliver community HIV testing, counseling, and care, updating patient records and home visit data with app-guided prompts.
  • Mentor Mothers follow up with mothers and children recently diagnosed with HIV, often serving as the first line of counselling after a positive result.

The design leans on CommCare’s core strengths: clinical decision support tailored to patient-specific diagnoses, longitudinal tracking across the full patient journey, multimedia audio and video prompts that reinforce training, and integration with OpenMRS, which the Ministry of Health uses at the facility level.

Frontline reach
1,750+ health workers

Infomovel supported more than 1,750 community health workers across seven provinces of Mozambique, with ten US government partners funded by CDC and USAID running on the same platform.

Standardization at national scale

The standardized implementation model carried its own outsized potential. By creating one shared app for HIV and TB community workflows, partners that had operated in silos began sharing knowledge and aligning to national protocols. Patients could now be referred across geographical regions and across different programmes.

HIV and TB data accuracy and accessibility improved across six regions of Mozambique, enabling more informed decisions at the facility, provincial, and national level. A machine learning program tested with Infomovel went further still, flagging the subset of patients at highest risk of defaulting from care so that limited follow-up effort could reach the people who needed it most.

By standardizing HIV and TB community workflows on a single CommCare platform, Infomovel turned fragmented, paper-based programs into a coordinated national effort toward the 90-90-90 targets.
