About this episode

In part one of a five-part series marking Dimagi's 20th anniversary, Jonathan Jackson sits down with four early employees who were most involved in creating CommCare, the low-code platform that lets NGOs and governments build applications for their frontline teams. Used by a million people across more than 130 countries, CommCare was built over 14 years through an unusual story of tenacity, humility, and continuous learning from users.

The conversation traces Dimagi's earliest wild west days of bespoke, custom-coded projects, the realization that every one of those projects created a long maintenance burden, and the gradual, often skeptical shift toward a repeatable product. Along the way the team unpacks the move from windows mobile PDAs to Nokia brick phones, the offline-first instincts forged by building software in remote settings, and the participatory design sessions held literally under a mango tree south of Dar es Salaam.

You will hear how multimedia features added so low-literacy users could use CommCare were unexpectedly turned by health workers into behavior change tools, how case management and tracking data over time set CommCare apart from peers, and how it eventually became a product that non-engineers could build on. Speaking are Dr. Neal Lesh, Dr. Brian DeRenzi, Cory Zue, and Clayton Sims, sharing the pivots, disagreements, and lessons from the making of CommCare.

In this episode

  • The wild west years: how Dimagi's early bespoke projects each became a long-tail maintenance burden that made a product approach feel necessary
  • Design under the mango tree: participatory design sessions held literally under a tree south of Dar es Salaam, with users driving the decisions
  • Feeling the pain of users: building offline-first because the team faced the same connectivity limits as the field, sharing code over a local repo
  • Underestimating users: the client who noticed a swapped phone and asked where his data went, a lasting lesson on privacy and sophistication
  • Multimedia's surprise: audio and images added for low-literacy workers became behavior change tools when health workers showed them to clients
  • From config files to a product: the long, nonlinear path from hand-built XML forms to an app builder anyone could use on CommCare HQ
  • Going deep to go wide: why tracking data over time and case management, hard problems most products would skip, became CommCare's secret sauce
