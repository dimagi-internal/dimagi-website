Undersupported, Unrecognized and Vital: 3 Community Health Workers Supported by Lwala Community Alliance Speak Out

Part two of our Lwala Community Alliance series, told in their own voices: three Community Health Workers in Migori County, Kenya describe their journey into the work, a day in the field, and why CHWs deserve to be salaried, trained, and recognized.

About this episode

Jonathan Jackson and Amie Vaccaro sit down with three Community Health Workers supported by the Lwala Community Alliance in Migori County, Kenya: Millicent Miruka, Lawrence Oggo, and Jared Oola. This is part two of a two-part series on Lwala's work transforming the community health landscape in Kenya, following an earlier conversation with the organization's co-CEO.

The three CHWs share what first drew them to community health, including deeply personal experiences of loss and illness in their own families. They walk through a typical day in the field, from packing a bag of malaria tests, medications, and screening tools to prioritizing visits to pregnant mothers, under-five children, HIV clients, and the elderly across dozens of households.

They also speak candidly about what they love, the difficult clients and long days that wear on them, and how a digital job aid has made their work lighter and more reliable. Above all, they make a direct appeal to funders, implementers, and policymakers: Community Health Workers do skilled, essential work and deserve to be paid, trained, supervised, and recognized.

“We need to be to look like health workers, health caregivers who need to be salaried. We need to be recognized also. We should not be a tokenism. We should not be manipulated.”

Jared Oola, Community Health Worker

In this episode

  • Why they do this work: three first-hand stories of loss and survival that turned personal hardship into a calling for community health
  • A day in the field: packing the bag, prioritizing pregnant mothers, under-five children, HIV clients, and the elderly across 10 to 15 households a day
  • The hardest part: reaching difficult clients and farmers away in their fields, rescheduling visits, and working through rain and long days
  • The case for salaries: why CHWs, the front liners during Covid-19 and called out even at night, must be paid and not asked to volunteer
  • From volunteer to supported: Jared on years of unpaid work before Lwala began paying CHWs every month
  • Digital tools that help: how a CommCare job aid replaced bulky paperwork, prompts follow-up visits, and lets them sync reports from the household
  • A message to global health: be trained, paid, supervised, advanced, and recognized by ministries and governments
