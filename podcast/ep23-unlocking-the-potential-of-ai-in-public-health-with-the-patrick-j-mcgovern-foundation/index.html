About this episode

Recorded at the 2022 Global Digital Health Forum, this episode features Jonathan Jackson in conversation with Rebecca Distler, Strategist for AI, Data and Digital Health at the Patrick J. McGovern Foundation, an organization focused on advancing artificial intelligence and data science for public good and social impact.

Their conversation moves past the AI hype to the enabling environment underneath it: the decision making cultures, data standards and interoperability, and digital public infrastructure that determine whether innovative tools ever create real value. Distler and Jackson dig into why success is less about reaching scale and more about operating and improving at scale, and why architectural choices made up front matter so much more than a successful pilot.

Throughout, they keep returning to people. Health workers need to be paid, motivated, and supported, with digital woven into a real career pathway, and health systems need to put the person at the center rather than the disease. They close on a hard truth: surfacing an AI insight is only the first step, and impact depends on whether someone has the systems, the time, and the pathway to act on it.

“Success is not necessarily about getting to scale, but about operating and improving at scale.”

Rebecca Distler, on what to design for

In this episode

  • Invest in the enabling environment: why decision making, data standards, and interoperability matter more than any single tool
  • Operating at scale, not just reaching it: the architectural choices that have to be made up front
  • Digital public infrastructure meets health: lessons from COVID-19 vaccine certificates and digital ID
  • Thinking about day 2000 on day one: building for the long run instead of the pilot
  • Health is digital: why digital health should not be treated as separate from healthcare
  • People at the center, not the disease: building disease-agnostic, health-sensitive infrastructure
  • AI for public health, not just medicine: surfacing an insight is only useful if a health worker can act on it
