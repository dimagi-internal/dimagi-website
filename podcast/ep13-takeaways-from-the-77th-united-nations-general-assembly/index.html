About this episode

What is the UN General Assembly, and why does it matter for global health? Jonathan Jackson and Dr. Kelly Collins, Dimagi's Global Director of Digital Adherence, both attended the 77th UNGA in New York and join Amie Vaccaro to share their key takeaways from the week. They cover why Dimagi chose to attend, the high density of social entrepreneurs, implementers, and government partners in one place, and the advocacy happening around community health workers and the big three diseases of HIV, TB, and malaria.

A central theme is the urgent need to break down the institutionalized silos that have long shaped global health, funding HIV, TB, or malaria in isolation, and to move toward integrated, person-centered care that stretches every dollar further. They discuss COVID-19's reversal of nearly two decades of progress, the challenge of earmarked aid, and the Global Fund's 7th Replenishment, where Dimagi made a $5 million matching commitment. The conversation closes on global development's talent gap and why the field needs more of the best and brightest, including people who do not think like the existing community.

“Us as innovators really need to push the donor community to start talking about funding and investment in a way that is more platform focused or more system focused.”

Dr. Kelly Collins, on breaking down silos

In this episode

  • Why UNGA matters: the one week when development professionals, funders, and governments are all in one place, and why Dimagi chose to attend
  • Breaking down silos: moving global health away from funding TB, HIV, or malaria in isolation toward integrated, person-centered care
  • COVID's setback: the first reversal of progress on HIV, TB, and malaria in nearly two decades, and what it demands of the field
  • Earmarked aid: why so much donor funding is tagged to specific diseases, and a little empathy for the donors caught in those mandates
  • The Global Fund's 7th Replenishment: what the multilateral fund does, the climate it fundraised in, and what happened in the room
  • Dimagi's $5M commitment: a matching fund to help countries invest Global Fund allocations in digital frontline worker programs
  • The talent gap: why global development needs more of the best and brightest, including people who think differently than the current community
