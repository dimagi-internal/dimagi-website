Equity and AI in Global Health: Exploring Large Language Models, Building Chatbots and Embracing Discomfort

Dimagi's Brian DeRenzi joins John Jackson and Amie Vaccaro to explore how GPT-4 and large language models could advance equity in global health, from natural-language SMS to voice for non-literate populations, while embracing the discomfort of this moment.

About this episode

John Jackson and Amie Vaccaro sit down with Brian DeRenzi, who leads Dimagi's Research and Data team, to talk through Dimagi's early work exploring large language models to build chatbots for global health and development. A self-described skeptic at first, Brian describes the aha moment that changed his mind: getting GPT-4 to read and write Sheng, a mix of Swahili and English spoken in Nairobi, with a degree of accuracy he was not expecting. The team is building single-use-case bots and evaluating them against four metrics: utility, accuracy, adherence, and safety.

The conversation digs into how AI might increase equity even as it risks decreasing it. Because bots can now operate on natural language rather than rigid numbered menus, SMS becomes far more useful and reaches people left behind by smartphone-only chatbots. Voice to text could open services to non-literate populations, an approach friends at Digital Green are piloting with their Farm Chat product in Hindi. The group also weighs harder questions: market dynamics that favor big tech, the risk that good-enough AI lets society off the hook on equity, and the irreplaceable value of human-to-human connection in care.

Throughout, Brian and John return to the theme of sitting with discomfort and uncertainty. They compare this moment to the early days of the pandemic, when no one knew which way things would go, and argue that the way to avoid a dystopian outcome is to keep building while actively keeping marginalized communities top of mind. As Amie summarizes, now is the time to start paying attention, and the value of human connection must remain paramount.

“We don't want to be transitioning towards a world where the only form of informational and emotional support that the world's marginalized communities are allowed to use come from GPT four.”

Brian DeRenzi, on the risk of leaving communities behind

In this episode

  • The aha moment: getting GPT-4 to read and write Sheng, a blend of Swahili and English spoken in Nairobi, with surprising accuracy
  • Four metrics for a good bot: how the team evaluates utility, accuracy, adherence, and safety on single-use-case chatbots
  • SMS, reborn: why natural language frees users from long numbered menus and reaches people without smartphones
  • Voice for non-literate populations: speech to text as an equity unlock, including Digital Green's Farm Chat work in Hindi
  • Equity cuts both ways: how to keep the AI revolution from making the already-productive richer while leaving marginalized communities behind
  • Healthcare between humans: why human-to-human connection stays irreplaceable, and AI should support frontline workers, not replace them
  • Embracing the discomfort: sitting with uncertainty, like the early pandemic, as the way to steer AI toward positive impact
