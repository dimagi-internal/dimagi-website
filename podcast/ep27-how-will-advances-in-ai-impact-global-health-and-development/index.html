How Will Advances in AI Impact Global Health and Development with Clayton Sims

Recorded at the Global Digital Health Forum, Dimagi CTO Clayton Sims joins John Jackson to separate the AI hype from the practical, from language models and biometrics to copyright, on-prem hosting, and what it all means for global health and development.

About this episode

Recorded live at the Global Digital Health Forum, John Jackson sits down with Clayton Sims, Dimagi's Chief Technology Officer, to talk through the opportunities, realities, and open questions of artificial intelligence in global health and development. Returning for a second appearance on the show, Clayton reflects on how the field has matured since AI was last a hot topic at the forum, and why this year the conversation finally moved from abstract excitement to practical, everyday use.

The two explore where AI already touches Dimagi's work, from threat detection on servers to the bias baked into models trained on what counts as a normal business, and where it could genuinely help: language models that reach frontline workers in their own languages, voice to text that maximizes time with patients, and image processing and biometrics on increasingly capable devices. Clayton is candid about the limits too, including the conservative guardrails of tools like ChatGPT and whether global health use cases will survive them.

The conversation closes on harder structural questions: the copyright and IP implications of generative models trained on people's code and art, the risk that AI pushes people to stop sharing openly, and the infrastructure reality that training modern models on-prem is nearly impossible. Clayton argues the path forward is not a binary cloud versus on-prem choice, but a thoughtful approach to when and how to use the cloud while keeping private data protected.

“I don't want AI tools to be the reason people stop being open about what they share, and that to me feels like a real loss.”

Clayton Sims, on open source and generative AI

In this episode

  • Hype versus practice: why AI finally felt mature at this year's Global Digital Health Forum after years of unmet excitement
  • AI you don't notice: how Dimagi already relies on it for server threat detection, and the bias of models that flag any server not in a US data center
  • Language as the differentiator: translation and chatbots that could let small organizations reach frontline workers in their own languages, from Malagasy onward
  • Conservative guardrails: the risk that safety filters on tools like ChatGPT quietly cut out the very global health topics that matter most
  • Copyright and open source: what generative models trained on people's code and art mean for IP, fair use, and the willingness to share openly
  • The on-prem problem: why training modern AI models on local hardware is nearly impossible, and how to rethink cloud use without giving up data ownership
  • Biometrics and image processing: where on-device vision and tensor processing are heading, and when they cross the line to being useful
