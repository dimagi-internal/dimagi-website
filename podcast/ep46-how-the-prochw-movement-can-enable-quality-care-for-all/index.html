About this episode

Community health workers are proven, again and again, to be the best way to get care to the poor, the rural, the stigmatized, and the underserved. Yet millions are not salaried, skilled, supervised, or supplied. The WHO recommends remunerating CHWs, but more than 80 percent of those in Africa are unsalaried, and most are women. CHWs also face essential medicine stock-outs about a third of the time, leaving them less able to treat common causes of illness and death like malaria, diarrhea, and pneumonia.

Dr. Madeleine Ballard, CEO of the Community Health Impact Coalition (CHIC), joins Amie Vaccaro and Jonathan Jackson to make the case for professional CHWs (proCHWs). CHIC is a coalition of CHWs and aligned health organizations in about 40 countries working to make professional CHWs the norm by changing guidelines, funding, and policy. Ballard explains the countries leading the way, why female labor has been treated as cheap and optional, and how CHIC's policy dashboard tracks progress across the 137 countdown countries.

The conversation also confronts the role of technology, which Ballard insists is never neutral. Used well it can make health workers' jobs better, surface and dignify their labor, and even grow the movement, as a free digital advocacy course has done by reaching thousands of CHWs. Used poorly it becomes extractive. The episode closes with a clear call to action: join the movement for proCHWs so that everyone, including those who provide care, has the support they need.
