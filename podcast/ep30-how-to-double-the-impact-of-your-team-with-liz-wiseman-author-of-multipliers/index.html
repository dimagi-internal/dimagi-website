About this episode

Jonathan Jackson and Amie Vaccaro sit down with Liz Wiseman, researcher, executive advisor, and author of the New York Times bestseller Multipliers, to explore what she calls multiplier leadership. Drawing on research that began with her years at Oracle, Liz explains the difference between diminishers, who run a one big brain model and access only about half of their people's intelligence, and multipliers, who invite and amplify the intelligence of those around them.

Much of the conversation centers on the accidental diminisher: the well-meaning, hyperactive leader whose strengths, like optimism, responsiveness, and a need for speed, end up crowding out the contributions of their team. Liz offers practical ways through this, from the player coach lesson she draws from Magic Johnson to sizing challenges smaller for newcomers and naming the difference between freeways and playgrounds where mistakes are safe.

The hosts press Liz on how this applies in resource-constrained global health and development work, where leaders are often both managing and contributing as individuals. Her research finds the diminishing dynamic can be even stronger in sacred and mission-driven organizations, where noble intent justifies hyperactive leadership. Her counterintuitive remedy: make your accidental diminisher tendencies a public, even laughable, conversation so the whole team helps keep them in check.

“Most diminishing is coming from what I call the accidental diminisher. The person who really wants to be a good boss, who wants to use people's strengths, but yet even with the best of intentions and with seemingly good leadership strategies are having a diminishing effect.”

Liz Wiseman

In this episode

  • Diminishers vs. multipliers: why the one big brain model gets half of people's intelligence, while multipliers get twice as much
  • The accidental diminisher: how strengths like optimism, responsiveness, and high standards quietly shut down the people around you
  • The player coach problem: Liz's Magic Johnson story on how to play big in a way that lets everyone else play big too
  • Multiplying newcomers: smaller two-week bites instead of two-year projects, and naming where it's safe to experiment
  • Why speed diminishes: the do-it-yourself tendency, and the upfront investment in defining what good looks like that pays time rebates later
  • The mission-driven trap: why diminishing runs strongest in nonprofits, schools, and churches where noble intent excuses hyperactive leadership
  • A culture fix: making your diminisher tendencies public so your whole team helps keep them in check
