About this episode

In the first part of Dimagi's founding story, co-host Amie Vaccaro talks with co-founder Dr. Vikram Kumar and co-founder and CEO Jonathan Jackson about the company's earliest days. Vikram, a medical student working at the MIT Media Lab, wanted to surface healthcare problems so engineers could solve them at scale, an idea that became Dimagi, a name drawn from the Hindi word for brain. Alongside co-founders Vishwanath and Tarjay Mickelson, the team began developing early work to improve rural healthcare, starting in India with the All India Institute of Medical Sciences.

Jonathan, a 22-year-old MIT computer science graduate searching for fulfilling work, met Vikram through an MIT development ventures club led by a figure named Yost. Within seconds of their first conversation, Jonathan knew he had found his calling and effectively hired himself. Within about a year, he proposed to Vikram that he become CEO, and Vikram supported the quick transition. Three weeks after joining, Jonathan took his first trip to Africa to lead Dimagi's first major project: a PEPFAR-funded national medical record system in Zambia.

That Zambia project shaped Dimagi's enduring approach, including the field-driven user-centered design the team once called designing for the mango tree. It also led to a defining tough decision: when the project's heavy focus on central data collection diverged from Dimagi's frontline-worker-centric vision, the team chose to walk away, giving up half its revenue at the time. The company recovered that revenue within the year and grew faster by better aligning impact with its partners.
