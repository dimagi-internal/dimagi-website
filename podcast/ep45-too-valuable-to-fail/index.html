Too Valuable to Fail

A short Your Questions Answered episode unpacking a learning Jonathan Jackson shared on LinkedIn: after two decades in digital health, the goal is not to be too big to fail, but too valuable to fail.

About this episode

In this Your Questions Answered episode, hosts Amie Vaccaro and Jonathan Jackson try a short form, video first format built around questions from the audience. The conversation springs from a LinkedIn post by Jonathan: after twenty years of working with governments on digital health programs, he no longer believes the goal is to get too big to fail. The only path to success, he argues, is to be too valuable to fail.

The two discuss what it means to shift from the language of users and scale to the language of value, and why partnering on the whole problem, including service delivery, training, and frontline worker resilience, matters more than simply turning a system on. Jonathan points to deep government partnerships in Burkina Faso and Ethiopia as examples of programs designed to keep creating value over time, never to be stagnant from day one.

Along the way they introduce Impact Delivery, Dimagi's evolving methodology for blending vertical, single disease funding with horizontal, platform value within the real constraints of government procurement and donor funding cycles. They close on measurement, from randomized controlled trials to product feedback, including the new WellMe resilience product Dimagi is building with content validated by Johnson and Johnson.

“Nothing's too big to fail. Governments are incredibly busy, have lots of competing priorities, lots of options. And you have to win the argument that you're too valuable to fail.”

Jonathan Jackson

In this episode

  • Too big to fail vs. too valuable to fail: why scale and user counts do not make a digital system irreplaceable, and what does
  • The language of value: measuring success by improved health and agricultural outcomes, not by whether a system got deployed or trained
  • Partnering on the whole problem: owning the outcome with governments rather than checking off a single task
  • Burkina Faso and Ethiopia: deep, government led partnerships designed to keep adding value, including a paperless Bereda pilot and a cost effectiveness study
  • Impact Delivery: blending vertical, single disease funding with horizontal platform value within real procurement and funding constraints
  • Measuring real value: RCTs, pre post surveys, and asking whether the actual product, not the idealized one, is creating a better job for the user
  • The WellMe example: launching a frontline worker resilience product with content validated by Johnson and Johnson, paired with rigorous research
