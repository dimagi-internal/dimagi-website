About this episode

Our co-hosts are joined by Chris Lochhead, a thirteen-time number one bestselling co-author and a pioneer of Category Design, to unpack what the discipline is and why it matters for NGOs and social impact organizations. Lochhead traces his path from getting thrown out of school at 18 to three turns as a Silicon Valley CMO and co-founding Play Bigger, the category design consulting firm behind the book that named the field.

The conversation centers on a core idea: everything is the way it is because someone legendary changed the way it was. Category designers do not compete in an existing market, they create a new one by naming, framing, and claiming a problem so compellingly that other people come to see it as their own. Lochhead argues impact organizations too often pitch their solution and ask for money, when the unlock is to make the audience care about the problem first.

Through stories ranging from the women's movement and its rally cry of equal pay for equal work to the Save West Cliff coastal restoration effort in Santa Cruz, Lochhead and the hosts explore rejecting the premise, standing in the future to pull the present forward, and the power of new language. Jonathan Jackson connects it all to Dimagi's own category of impact delivery and the challenge of leading a field you helped build toward a new point of view.

“When you market your product, I think you want my money. When you market my problem, I think you want to help me.”

Chris Lochhead

In this episode

  • What Category Design is: a management discipline for anyone doing breakthrough work, premised on the idea that everything is the way it is because someone legendary changed it
  • Creating, not competing: why legendary change agents from Martin Luther King to Bob Marley fought the status quo rather than a rival
  • Market the problem, not the product: making your problem the audience's problem so they are compelled to act
  • Equal pay for equal work: how a single provocative point of view turned a women's issue into a human one and changed the law
  • Save West Cliff: reframing a fight over a road into a 50-year plan for natural feature restoration on the Santa Cruz coast
  • Reject the premise and back-cast: standing in a radically different future and pulling the present forward, rather than forecasting from today
  • The power of language: why you cannot describe an exponential future with words from the past, from Starbucks to impact delivery
