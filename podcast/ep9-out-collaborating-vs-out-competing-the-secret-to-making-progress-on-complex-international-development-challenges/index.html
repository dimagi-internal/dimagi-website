About this episode

Jonathan Jackson and Gillian Javetski, Dimagi's Chief of Staff, sit down with Amie Vaccaro to discuss why making progress on complex international development and global health challenges depends on collaboration rather than competition. With resources scarce and projects involving many stakeholders, governments, funders, and implementing partners, they argue your core skill becomes how well you collaborate, not how well you compete.

Drawing on decades of Dimagi experience, including the COVID vaccine response work with Ona and Medic and the early Open Rosa and JavaRosa open source communities, the conversation unpacks what distinguishes good collaboration from bad. The healthiest collaborations, they argue, are selfish and built around a shared problem both organizations genuinely need to solve, and the strongest ones level up from the transactional task to the higher level outcome the public sector and patients actually want.

Gillian and Jonathan also confront a paradox: despite a deeply collaborative culture, people have called Dimagi uncollaborative. They draw a line between knowledge sharing and true collaboration toward a shared goal, explain why Dimagi declines meetings that exist only for status updates, and reflect on when to re-evaluate or end a collaboration that has run its course.

“Collaboration for the sake of collaboration is a terrible idea. It never works. So the healthiest collaborations are selfish. Your organization was going to try to solve this problem anyways, and you are selfishly coming into the collaboration, recognizing you have a big thing you want to gain from it.”

Jonathan Jackson

  • Good vs bad collaboration: the difference between advocating for shared change and just showing up to a working group to give status updates
  • The selfish collaboration: why the healthiest partnerships start from a problem your organization genuinely needs to solve
  • Leveling up: stepping above the transactional task to the higher level outcome the public sector and patients really want
