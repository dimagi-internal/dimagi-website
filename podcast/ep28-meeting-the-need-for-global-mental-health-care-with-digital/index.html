About this episode

Recorded on the sidelines of the Global Digital Health Forum, this episode features Dimagi CEO Jonathan Jackson in conversation with Lauren Magoun, Dimagi's Senior Health Strategy Specialist on the research and data team. Lauren spends most of her time researching how Dimagi can improve access to and quality of mental health care around the world.

Together they explore the staggering unmet need for mental health care, especially in lower income countries where specialists like psychiatrists and psychologists are scarce. Lauren explains how brief, structured psychological interventions delivered by non-specialists, including community health workers, nurses, and lay counselors, have shown real outcomes in treating depression and anxiety, and why a protocolized approach can make these models work.

The conversation turns to the role of technology in scaling training and supervision, supporting providers with digital job aids, collecting data for learning, and reaching clients between sessions. Lauren and Jonathan also discuss the high comorbidity of mental illness with conditions like HIV and TB, and why Dimagi's focus is on augmenting human providers rather than replacing them.

“There actually are many approaches you can take to help many people that don't require that level of training of the healthcare workers.”

Lauren Magoun, on closing the specialist gap

In this episode

  • The staggering unmet need for mental health care, and why so few people in lower income countries have any access to it
  • Non-specialist delivery: how community health workers, nurses, and lay counselors can deliver structured brief interventions with good outcomes
  • Why brief models work: clear training protocols, reframing thoughts, and the power of someone who simply listens over six to eight sessions
  • Where digital fits: scaling training and supervision, digital job aids, data collection, and client-facing support between sessions
  • Augmentation, not replacement: why Dimagi supports human providers rather than going fully app-only
  • Mental health as comorbidity: integrating it into HIV and TB care, and its links to education and income
  • Adolescent mental health and resiliency: spotting severe illness early, plus supporting burned-out frontline workforces
