About this episode

Half the world's population lacks access to essential health services, and community health workers who bring care door to door in the hardest to reach places are an essential part of closing that gap. Yet despite a return on investment that has held at roughly 10 to 1, financing remains the primary barrier to scaling and sustaining these programs.

In this conversation, co-hosts Jonathan Jackson and Amie Vaccaro are joined by Nan Chen, Co-Executive Director of Africa Frontline First, a partnership of the Community Health Impact Coalition, the Financing Alliance for Health, and Last Mile Health. Born out of the COVID-19 moment at the urging of former Liberian president Ellen Johnson Sirleaf, the organization works to get more money to community health workers and to make existing money flow more effectively.

Nan traces the shift from arguing whether countries should invest in community health to figuring out how, and digs into the unglamorous technical assistance, resource mapping, and relationship building that puts ministries of health in the driver's seat. The discussion also turns to the role of digital tools, payments, and Dimagi's Learn, Deliver, Verify, and Pay model in bringing program costs down.

“Community health workers are cost effective. But they aren't cheap. And that's not a bad thing. Services and quality costs money.”

Nan Chen, Africa Frontline First

In this episode

  • From whether to how: over 40 African countries now recognize professional community health workers in policy, but fewer than 10 have a budget line for them
  • The origin story: how the COVID moment and former president Ellen Johnson Sirleaf set Africa Frontline First in motion
  • Government in the driver's seat: why resource mapping and Excel spreadsheets make ministries deal makers, not deal takers
  • Making programs politics proof: institutionalizing community health so it survives changes in leadership and becomes routine
  • Listening to commitment: the collective financing approach behind one plan, one budget, one M&E report
  • Learn, Deliver, Verify, Pay: how digital tools and faster payments can lower the cost of running CHW programs
  • Domestic ownership: the hard fiscal and political choice of moving health worker salaries onto government payrolls
